I sell website builds, so read this knowing that. I'll still give you the real answer, because pushing DIY-shaped businesses into paid builds creates unhappy clients and pushing pro-shaped businesses into DIY creates slow failures.

## When DIY wins outright

Pre-revenue idea tests. Hobby and community projects. Businesses whose customers all arrive by referral and just need an address to verify you exist. In those cases a template with real photos and honest copy beats an expensive build, and [when DIY is right](/guides/when-diy-is-right) walks how to do it well. On picking a builder, [Wix or Squarespace vs custom](/guides/wix-squarespace-vs-custom) sorts who should pick what.

## When DIY quietly fails

The pattern is always the same: the template looked great in the demo, then real content went in, then it got slow, then weekends disappeared into settings panels, and a year later the site exists but doesn't produce. Templates have a ceiling and [here's where it sits](/guides/website-templates-limits). The technical debt isn't the worst part; the invisible part is conversion, where DIY sites almost never get engineering attention at all.

## What you're actually buying when you hire

Not pixels. A [good designer's real work](/guides/what-a-web-designer-actually-does) is deciding what the site says, in what order, to move a stranger toward contact, plus the engineering that makes it fast and findable. [The full breakdown](/guides/diy-website-vs-professional) compares both paths cost-for-cost, including your hours.

## Decide with data

Run your current site (DIY or otherwise) through the [Website Leak Scanner](/tools/website-leak-scanner). If it comes back clean, keep the money. If it comes back bleeding, you'll know exactly what you'd be paying to fix.
