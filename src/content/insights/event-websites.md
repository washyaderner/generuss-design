Normal websites can improve gradually. An event site can't; every day it underperforms is inventory you never sell again. That deadline changes how the site should be built and when.

## The job, in order

Above the fold: event name, date, city, one line on who it's for, and the ticket button. Visitors decide on events fast and share them faster, so clarity beats cleverness harder here than anywhere. Below that: the speaker or program lineup that justifies the price, proof from past years if they exist, logistics answered before they become support emails, and a checkout that doesn't lose people at the last step. The full [event website checklist](/guides/event-website-checklist) covers what sells tickets, and the [mistakes list](/guides/conference-website-mistakes) covers what quietly kills them.

## Timing is half the build

Sites that launch late fight physics: momentum, early-bird pricing, and word of mouth all need runway. [When to launch](/guides/event-website-timeline) lays out a timeline that works backward from your date. On selling direct versus platforms like Eventbrite, the answer is usually both, with your own site as the hub: [own site vs platforms](/guides/sell-tickets-online-event-site) explains the tradeoff.
