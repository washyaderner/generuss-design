Redesigns are the most oversold product in web work. Sometimes they're exactly right. The skill is telling the difference before the invoice, not after.

## Real signals versus restlessness

Real: the site produces measurably fewer leads than it used to, it's broken or ugly on the phones most visitors use, you hide the URL in conversations, the builder platform blocks features you now need, or the business itself repositioned and the site sells the old one. Restlessness: a competitor launched something shiny, or you're just tired of looking at it. [The seven real signals](/guides/when-to-redesign-website) goes deeper on each.

## The cheaper move first

Many "redesign" problems are actually three fixable leaks: speed, an unclear homepage, and a weak contact flow. A [refresh instead of a rebuild](/guides/redesign-vs-refresh) tackles those on the existing site for a fraction of the cost, and [always audit before you redesign](/guides/website-audit-before-redesign) explains why I won't quote a rebuild without one. When you do rebuild, some things should survive the move: [what to keep](/guides/redesign-what-to-keep) covers the content and URLs that carry your accumulated equity.

## The rankings question

The redesign horror story is the business that rebuilt and vanished from Google. It happens when URLs change without redirects and content gets thinned out during the move. It is entirely preventable with a migration checklist, covered in [will a redesign hurt my SEO](/guides/website-redesign-seo).

The [Website Leak Scanner](/tools/website-leak-scanner) is the audit-first move in tool form: it tells you whether you need a rebuild or a fix list, free, before anyone quotes you anything.
