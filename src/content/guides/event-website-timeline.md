Launch your event website as early as you can defend that the event is real, and work every other date backward from the day it happens. The page doesn't need to be finished to go live; it needs to be sellable. Early-bird pricing, word of mouth, and speaker sharing all need runway, and runway is the one thing you can't buy back once the date is close.

## Work backward from the date

Start at the event date and count back. Ticket sales tend to arrive in two waves: an early one when you launch and announce, and a late one in the final stretch before the date. The long middle is where momentum either compounds or dies. If you only give yourself a few weeks, you get one weak wave and no middle to build on. Working backward, most events want the site live months ahead, not weeks, so there's room for both waves and everything in between them.

## Early-bird pricing needs runway

Early-bird pricing only works if there's enough time between the discount and the deadline for people to notice, decide, and act. A discount that opens and closes in the same two weeks isn't an early bird, it's a coincidence. Give the early rate real room: announce it at launch, remind people at the midpoint, and close it with enough time left that the full-price window still has a job to do. Your pricing calendar is a marketing tool, not just a number on a page.

## Launch a minimum sellable page first

The mistake that delays most event sites is waiting for the full schedule. You don't need it. A minimum sellable page has the essentials above the fold, a working buy button, and enough about the event to justify the ticket: the date, the city, who it's for, and one or two confirmed draws. Ship that the moment the event is confirmed. A live page selling tickets with a half-finished schedule beats a perfect page that launches too late to matter. The [event website checklist](/guides/event-website-checklist) covers what that first version must include.

## Let the rest land later

Once the page is live and selling, everything else arrives on its own schedule: the full speaker lineup, session times, sponsor logos, travel and hotel details, the FAQ that grows as questions come in. Publishing in stages is a feature, not a compromise. Each addition is a reason to email your list again and a fresh thing to share, so a page that fills in over time gives you more marketing moments than one big reveal ever could.

## Budget and timing move together

Timing also shapes what you should build. A last-minute event is better served by a focused landing page than an ambitious hub you can't finish in time, while an event with real runway can support a fuller site that earns its cost. Match the ambition of the build to the calendar you actually have, not the one you wish you had.
