Redesign when your website is failing at a measurable job, not when it feels tired. The real test is whether you can name the specific thing a rebuild would fix: leads that dried up, credibility you lose in sales conversations, customers who can't use the site on a phone, or a platform that blocks the features you need. If you can point at the damage, a rebuild earns its cost. If the best reason you can find is that you're bored of looking at it, keep your money and read on.

## The seven signals worth acting on

Each of these is a job the site is failing. Run the self-check before you count it.

1. **Leads dropped and stayed down.** Pull the last twelve months of inquiries. A real decline holds across seasons and survives the obvious explanations. If the phone rings less and nothing else in the business changed, the site is the suspect.
2. **It fails on a phone.** Open your own site on your phone and try to book yourself. If you pinch, zoom, or give up, so does the majority of your traffic, because that's where most of it now lives.
3. **You hide the URL.** Notice whether you catch yourself saying "it's being redesigned" in a sales conversation. That flinch is real data. A site you're ashamed to send is costing you deals you never see.
4. **The platform fights you.** List the last three things you wanted to add and couldn't. If a page builder or theme is the reason, editing won't help; the ceiling is the tool itself.
5. **The business moved and the site didn't.** Read your homepage headline out loud. If it sells the company you were two years ago, wrong offer, wrong audience, or an old service up front, the site is quietly working against you.
6. **Speed decayed.** Sites get slower with age as images, plugins, and trackers pile up. Run a speed test on a mid-range phone, not your fast laptop. A page that loaded quickly at launch often crawls a year later.
7. **The path to becoming a customer is unclear.** Ask someone who's never seen it to hire you, using only the site. If they can't figure out what to click in under a minute, traffic is leaking straight out the bottom.

## Two of those urges will be fake

Not every itch to rebuild is a signal. Two fakes dress up as reasons. The first is competitor envy: someone launched something shiny and now yours looks dated by comparison. But "dated next to theirs" is not the same as "failing at its job." The second is boredom: you stare at your site far more than any customer, so it goes stale for you long before it does for them. A new template that makes rebuilding look easy is a close cousin, ease of building is not evidence that a rebuild is the right move. None of these point at damage. If you can't attach a lost lead, a broken screen, or a blocked feature to the feeling, it's restlessness, and restlessness is how businesses spend five figures moving a problem from one template to another. The tell is always the same: a real signal has a victim you can name, a lost lead or a broken screen, while restlessness only has a feeling.

## Measure before you decide

Every real signal above is measurable, which means you don't have to guess your way into a five-figure decision.
