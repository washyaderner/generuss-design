An event website sells tickets when its first screen answers five questions before anyone scrolls: what the event is, when it happens, what city it's in, who it's for, and where to buy. Nail those five and everything below the fold gets a fair chance. Miss one and you've handed a stranger a reason to hesitate, and on an event page hesitation is a closed tab you never reopen.

## The five that belong above the fold

Event name in plain words, not a theme nobody can parse. The date and the city, because those two facts decide whether a visitor can attend at all, and they decide it in about a second. One line naming who the event is for, so the right person feels recognized and the wrong person leaves before they cost you a support email. And a buy button that looks like one: strong contrast, a single verb, no hunting for it. People judge events fast and forward them faster, so plain clarity outsells atmosphere here more than on any other kind of site.

## Speakers and program are your price justification

Nobody pays for a date on a calendar. They pay for what happens on it. The speaker lineup, the session titles, the workshops, the names they recognize: that's the part that turns interest into a purchase, because it answers the only real objection, which is whether the ticket is worth the money. List real names and real topics, not filler like 'world-class speakers to be announced.' A vague program reads as an event that isn't sure of itself yet, and people don't prepay for uncertainty.

## Answer the logistics before they ask

Every question you don't answer on the page becomes an email, and every email is a person who was ready to buy and got interrupted. Parking, start and end times, what a ticket includes, the refund policy, whether meals are covered, how to get there: put them where a visitor can find them without leaving the page. A short FAQ near the checkout does more for sales than another banner. You're not writing a manual, you're removing the small frictions that let someone talk themselves out of it.

## Make checkout the easy part

You can win the whole page and still lose the sale in the last ten seconds. Every extra field, every forced account creation, every redirect to a checkout that looks nothing like your site is a place people drop. Ask for what you need to sell a ticket and nothing more. If you use an outside ticketing platform, make the handoff feel continuous instead of a jarring jump to a stranger's branding. The [mistakes that quietly kill sales](/guides/conference-website-mistakes) covers where this step goes wrong most.

## Build it to be shared

The best event marketing is one attendee texting a link to a friend. That only works if the link carries its own context: a title and image that make sense dropped into a group chat, a page that loads fast on the phone it opens on, and a date that's visible without pinching to zoom. A site built to be shared compounds; a site that assumes people arrive one at a time from an ad does not. Timing matters as much as the page, and [when to launch](/guides/event-website-timeline) lays out the runway you need for word of mouth to do its work.

## Check what you've got

If your event site is already live, the fastest audit is to open it on your phone and run the five-question test out loud. What, when, where, who, and how to buy, in five seconds, no scrolling. Anything you stumble on is a leak.
