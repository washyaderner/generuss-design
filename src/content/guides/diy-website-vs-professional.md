The honest way to compare doing it yourself against hiring a professional is to count what each really costs, and the cost of DIY is not zero, because your hours have a price. The rule that settles most cases: if the site's job is simple presence, build it yourself; if the site's job is revenue, hire. And there's a hybrid that beats both for a lot of businesses, which is to start DIY and bring in a professional the moment leads start carrying real money.

## Count your own hours

A template subscription looks like the cheap option because the sticker price is small. But the real cost is the sticker plus your time, and founders routinely spend thirty or forty hours building a site a professional ships in a week. Put a number on your hours. Say your time is worth $50 an hour, and a build that eats forty of them cost you $2,000 in time before the subscription. If those same forty hours could have closed two jobs worth $800 each, the DIY site cost you $3,600, not the price of a theme. The hours were always the expensive part; the subscription just hides them. And forty hours is the optimistic version, the one where nothing goes wrong. The realistic version includes the evenings lost to a layout that won't cooperate and the second attempt after the first one didn't look right, none of which shows up in the price of the theme.

## The real question is the site's job

Every site has one of two jobs. A presence site exists so people who already found you can confirm you're real and get your number, and a good template covers that. A revenue site has to find strangers, persuade them, and turn them into leads, which is engineering, not decoration. The mistake is using a presence-shaped tool for a revenue-shaped job and then wondering why the site is busy but the phone is quiet. Name the job first, then pick the tool. The trap is that a presence tool and a revenue tool look identical from the outside; both produce a nice-looking homepage. The difference stays invisible until you check whether strangers who land on it ever become customers, and by then you've already paid for the wrong one. The [what a good web designer does](/guides/what-a-web-designer-actually-does) guide covers what the revenue job actually involves.

## The hybrid path

For a lot of businesses the smart move isn't DIY or pro, it's DIY then pro. Build a clean one-page site yourself while you're testing the idea and every dollar counts. Then, once leads start carrying real money and the site's weaknesses are costing you sales you can name, hire out the revenue version. You'll brief the professional far better for having built the first one, and you'll spend money on a site that has a proven job instead of a hopeful one. You also avoid the worst outcome, which is paying for a full build before you know whether the business works, then rebuilding it anyway once you learn what customers actually respond to. [When DIY is right](/guides/when-diy-is-right) covers the first half of that path in depth.

## Decide with the numbers in front of you

Put both options on the table with the full cost of each, your hours included. A $500 Launch Page that ships in days can be cheaper than a 'free' template that eats a month of your evenings, once you actually price the evenings. The [compare page](/compare) lays hand-coded builds next to subscription platforms and templates so you can see the tradeoff cost-for-cost, and the [DIY vs hiring a pro](/insights/diy-vs-pro) pillar maps when each answer wins. The right call isn't a matter of pride in either direction; it's whichever one costs you less to reach the job the site has to do.
