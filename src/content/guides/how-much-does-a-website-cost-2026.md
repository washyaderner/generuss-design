A website in 2026 costs anywhere from near-zero to five figures, and for a service business the honest range is about $500 for a focused one-page site to $4,500 and up for a full custom build. Templates sit below that as a monthly rental; agencies sit above it. The number that's right for you isn't the average, it's set by one thing: what the site has to earn back.

## What's actually inside each band

**Template DIY, a monthly subscription.** Wix, Squarespace, and the other builders. You do the design, the writing, and the upkeep, and you pay the platform every month for as long as the site exists. The ceiling is whatever the template can do. It's the right answer for testing an idea or a business that doesn't need the site to bring in work.

**Focused launch site, around $500.** One page, built by a professional, engineered to make you bookable: a clear offer, real proof, and an obvious way to contact you. This is where most new service businesses should start, because it converts real leads without betting a full-build budget before demand is proven. The $500 Launch Page is this band.

**Full custom build, $4,500 and up.** Multiple pages built around how you actually get clients, with search visibility, fast load times, and conversion designed in rather than decorated on. This band earns its price when leads are worth real money and the site is a primary way you get them.

**Agency retainers, five figures and beyond.** Teams, meetings, and timelines. Sometimes worth it at serious scale. Often the same deliverable as the band below with more people attached to the invoice.

## How to pick your band

Don't start from the sticker price. Start from what a client is worth to you. Take your average customer value and your honest close rate, and ask what one additional booked client a month would mean. For a business where a client is worth real money, a higher band pays for itself quickly. For one where each job is small, an expensive build may never earn back, and a focused site is the smarter call.

This is why two businesses can get the same quote and only one should say yes. The price isn't good or bad on its own; it's good or bad relative to what your leads are worth. [Why website quotes vary](/guides/why-website-quotes-vary) breaks down what you're actually paying for, and [the ROI math](/guides/website-roi-math) turns "what a client is worth" into a number you can check against any quote.

A useful gut check: picture the site booking one extra client in its first month. If that single client would cover a real chunk of the price, you're in the right band. If you'd need the site to book steadily for a year just to break even, the band is too high for where your business is now, and starting smaller costs you nothing you can't add later. A site isn't a one-time decision; you can begin with a focused page and grow into a full build once demand is proven.

## What to ask before accepting any quote

Four questions expose what a quote really includes. Who writes the copy, you or them? Is the design custom, or a template you'll be filling in? Is anyone thinking about whether the site converts, or only about how it looks? And what happens after launch: who hosts it, who owns the files, who makes changes? A quote that goes quiet on these is cheaper for a reason, and the reason is usually work you'll end up doing yourself. Get the answers in writing before you pay, not after, because a scope that lived only in a friendly conversation has a way of shrinking once the invoice is sent.

Picking a band is really about matching what you spend to what the site has to return.
