Start small unless two things are already true: your leads are valuable and your demand is proven. That's the whole decision in one line. A one-page Launch Page makes you bookable cheaply and fast; a full build makes you found and bookable at scale for more. The instinct is to treat it as small versus big, but the better frame is order: which rung to stand on first. And because starting small credits toward the bigger build, choosing the first rung wrong is cheap to correct, while choosing to overbuild rarely is.

## Let lead value do the math

The number that should drive this is what one booked customer is worth to you. Run the real numbers. Say a job is worth $800 to your business and you close half the people who book a call. Then every booked call is worth $400, and a site that produces even a few extra a month pays for a full build quickly. Now say a job is worth $60 and closes rarely; the same site takes far longer to earn its cost, and starting small is clearly right. High lead value tilts you toward building the acquisition system sooner, because each customer it brings covers a lot of ground. Low lead value says prove the model cheaply first. Do that arithmetic before you look at any design, because it decides the rung more reliably than taste does.

## Proof of demand is the other input

Lead value tells you what a customer is worth; proof of demand tells you whether the customers are there at all. If you already have steady inquiries, repeat referrals, or a waitlist, demand is proven and a bigger build has something to amplify. If you're still testing whether people want what you sell, you don't yet know which searches to target or which offer lands, and a full build would be guessing in expensive detail. A one-page site is the cheap experiment that answers those questions: it shows you real interest and the words customers actually use. Buy the big build after the market has spoken, not before.

## The failure mode in each direction

Both wrong turns have a signature. Overbuying looks like a beautiful multi-page site sitting ahead of a business that can't yet feed it, cash tied up in pages and search terms you don't have the demand to use. It's the more expensive mistake because the money's already spent. Undershooting looks like outgrowing a one-page site and feeling capped: you've proven demand, being found across many searches is now the constraint, and a single page can't do that job.

Undershooting is cheaper to fix, though, precisely because the upgrade path exists. Starting small credits toward the full build, so correcting an undershoot is an upgrade, not a restart. That asymmetry, cheap to fix if you start small, costly if you overbuild, is the reason the default leans toward the Launch Page whenever the decision is close.
