A website's monthly cost comes in two very different shapes. With a builder like Wix or Squarespace, you pay a subscription every month for as long as the site is live, and that fee is the price of renting the platform. With a built-and-owned site, you pay a one-time cost to build it and then a small hosting bill, often just a few dollars a month and sometimes nothing. Over a few years, those two shapes add up to very different totals.

## The subscription model is a forever-fee

A builder plan feels cheap because the number is small and monthly. The catch is that it never ends. You pay it in year one and year five alike, and the day you stop paying, the site goes dark. You're not buying a website; you're renting access to one, and the meter runs for as long as your business does.

That's not automatically bad. Renting buys you convenience: templates, hosting, and updates bundled into one predictable bill, with no upfront outlay. For some businesses that trade is worth it. But it helps to see the subscription for what it is, a recurring cost with no finish line, rather than a one-time purchase.

## The ownership model front-loads the cost

A built site inverts the math. You pay more up front to have it made, then almost nothing to keep it running. A fast, modern site is just files served to visitors, so hosting can cost a few dollars a month or fall inside a free tier. You own those files. Nobody can switch the site off because a subscription lapsed, and moving it somewhere else is your decision, not a favor you have to ask for.

## The three-year math

Round numbers make the difference clear. Say a builder plan runs $30 a month, a middle-of-the-road figure. Over three years that's a bit over a thousand dollars, and at the end you own nothing; stop paying and the site disappears. A one-time $500 Launch Page plus near-free hosting lands around six hundred dollars total over the same three years, and the site is yours at the end of it.

The gap widens the longer the site lives. Year four, year five, the subscription keeps billing while the owned site keeps costing almost nothing. Renting is cheapest in month one and gets more expensive every month after; owning is most expensive in month one and gets cheaper every month after.

None of these numbers are yours exactly; drop in the real plan price and the real build cost and the shape holds. The subscription is a line that keeps climbing; the owned site is a line that goes nearly flat after the first payment. Somewhere in the first couple of years those two lines cross, and every month past the crossing is money the renter keeps paying and the owner keeps.

## When renting is still the right call

None of this means a subscription is a mistake. If you need a site live this week, can't put money down up front, or you're standing up a placeholder while you figure out the business, a builder is a reasonable choice. You trade long-term cost for short-term ease, and you keep the freedom to walk away whenever you want. It also matters who will maintain the site. If that person is you and a builder's bundled updates save you real time, the subscription is buying back your hours, which can be the best reason of all to rent.

The rule is about time horizon. If the site is a short-term or low-stakes presence, rent it. If it's a lasting part of how you get work, owning it usually costs less and traps you less.
