The fastest way to find out is to ask. Open ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Google's AI overview and type the question a customer would type: the best electrician in your city, or who should I hire to photograph a wedding this fall. If your name comes back, you're in the set the assistant recommends. If it doesn't, you've found a gap, and it's a fixable one, because these answers are assembled from sources you can influence. The whole check takes about ten minutes and needs nothing but the assistants themselves.

## The prompts your customers actually use

Customers don't ask in marketing language. They ask in three plain shapes. The first is local intent: best roofer near me, or house cleaning in your town. The second is hiring intent: who should I hire to fix a leaking shower, or who's the best caterer for a small wedding. The third is a trust check on a name they already have: is this company any good, is this company reputable. Run all three, reword them a few times, and try each with and without your city. Assistants rarely answer identically twice, so a single test isn't a verdict. Five or ten variations is, and the pattern across them is what you're reading for.

## Reading the three outcomes

Every result lands in one of three buckets, and each one means something different.

**Recommended.** You're named, ideally in a short list of two or three. That's the win. The next job is to understand why you earned it and defend the spot, so a competitor's push doesn't quietly replace you.

**Absent.** You aren't mentioned at all. This is the common case for local businesses that haven't done any of the work yet, and it's the one that costs the most, because absence never announces itself. The customer simply hires whoever got named.

**Misdescribed.** You're mentioned, but something is wrong: an old service area, hours you changed, a service you dropped, or a detail that belongs to a different business with a similar name. A confident wrong answer can be worse than silence, because it sends the right customer to the wrong conclusion.

## What feeds the answer

Assistants recommend what they can read and verify. That means your Google Business Profile, the review platforms, the directories that list you, and the words on your own site. Underneath all of it sits consistency: when the web agrees on your name, your service, and your location, the assistant trusts the pattern and repeats it. When your details conflict from one listing to the next, it hedges, and hedging usually means leaving you out. None of this is mysterious, and none of it is locked. It's the same foundation that decides ordinary search results, pointed at a new kind of answer. The pillar on [AI visibility](/insights/ai-visibility) shows how those layers stack up.

## Why the answer isn't the same every time

Don't read one result as final. Assistants pull from sources that change, they phrase things differently on each run, and they don't all draw on the same data, so ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's answer can disagree on the same question. That's why one test tells you little and a batch tells you a lot. Check across a few assistants, run several wordings, and note which names show up again and again. The businesses that appear consistently, across tools and across phrasings, are the ones that have made themselves easy to verify. Re-run the same batch every quarter, because the answer moves as your competitors do the work or fail to.

## Turn the check into a fix

Run your prompts, note the outcome for each, and screenshot the results so you can compare them month to month. If you're absent or misdescribed, the next move is structural: make your site easy for an assistant to quote, and make your facts agree everywhere they appear.
