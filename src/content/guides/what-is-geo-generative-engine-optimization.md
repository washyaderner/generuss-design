GEO, generative engine optimization, is the work of getting your business cited as a source when an AI assistant writes an answer. If AEO makes one page's answer easy to quote, GEO makes your whole business trustworthy enough that the model names you as where the answer came from. The difference matters: AEO operates on a page, GEO operates on your business as an entity across the entire web, which is a slower, deeper kind of work.

## What makes an assistant cite you

Three things decide whether a model treats you as a citable source, and all three come down to trust.

**Entity consistency.** Your name, address, phone, and service description should say the same thing everywhere they appear: your site, your Google profile, every directory. When those signals agree, the model reads a single confident entity. When they conflict, it reads noise and hedges, and a hedging model leaves you unnamed.

**Schema depth.** Structured data that goes past the basics. Not just that you exist, but your services, the areas you cover, your reviews, your FAQs. Depth gives a model more it can safely repeat, and safe-to-repeat is the whole game in citation.

**Verifiable facts.** Claims a machine can corroborate somewhere else. Twenty years in business is citable when the web backs it up; an unprovable superlative is not. Assistants lean on what they can confirm and quietly drop what they can't, so give them facts that check out.

## The foundation GEO sits on

None of this replaces the basics. Reviews, an accurate Google profile, and real content on your own site are the raw material a model reads before it decides whether to cite you. GEO is not a trick layered on a thin presence; it's what turns a solid presence into a cited one. If the foundation is missing, build that first, then make it consistent and machine-readable. Skipping straight to schema on top of an empty profile gives an assistant more structure but nothing worth repeating.

## GEO versus AEO, one clean distinction

Here's the cleanest way to hold the two apart. AEO makes the answer quotable. GEO makes your business the source that gets credited for it. AEO is page-level and about structure; GEO is entity-level and about trust and consistency. You want both, and they share a foundation, but they're not the same job. [AEO, explained plainly](/guides/what-is-aeo) covers the page side, and lightweight signals like [an llms.txt file](/guides/llms-txt-explained) sit on the GEO side, telling crawlers what your business is.

## What GEO is not

It helps to name the misconceptions. GEO is not keyword stuffing; repeating your city and service twenty times does nothing for a model deciding whether to trust you. It's not a one-time task you check off, because your listings drift, your hours change, and consistency has to be maintained. And it's not a replacement for doing good work that earns real reviews and real mentions. GEO organizes and clarifies a real presence so a machine can rely on it. If there's no real presence underneath, there's nothing to organize, and no amount of schema invents a reputation you haven't earned.

## Why starting early compounds

Citations reinforce themselves. Once a model has learned to trust and name a business, it tends to keep naming it, because consistency is exactly the signal it was looking for. That means the work compounds: the business that becomes a clean, verifiable entity now becomes a default answer later, while the field is still thin. Catching up after competitors have already become the names models reach for is slower and more expensive than getting there first.
