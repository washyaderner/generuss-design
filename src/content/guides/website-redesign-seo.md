A redesign hurts your SEO only if you let it, and the damage is almost always self-inflicted through three avoidable mistakes: changing URLs without redirects, thinning out content that was ranking, and dropping the internal links that tied your pages together. Handled with a migration checklist, a redesign is safe, and a faster, clearer site can rank better than the one it replaced. The horror stories come from teams that rebuilt the look and forgot the plumbing underneath it.

## Why rankings die in a migration

Search rankings aren't attached to your business. They're attached to specific pages at specific addresses. A rebuild that ignores that is how traffic disappears overnight.

- **Changed URLs with no redirects.** Every page you've ever ranked lives at a URL that search engines have indexed and other sites have linked to. If the new site moves that page to a new address and nothing points the old one forward, the page effectively dies, and its rankings die with it. This is the single biggest cause of post-redesign traffic loss.
- **Thinned content.** Redesigns love clean, minimal pages, and a lot of ranking content gets cut in the name of tidiness. The trouble is that search engines ranked those pages partly because of the words on them. Strip the words to make it pretty and you strip the reason it ranked.
- **Lost internal links.** The links between your pages tell search engines which pages matter and how they relate. A fresh build often rebuilds the navigation and forgets the in-content links, and pages that were held up by a web of internal links suddenly stand alone and slip.

## The migration checklist

None of that is hard to prevent. Before the new site goes live, walk this list.

1. Map every old URL to its new home, and set a redirect for any address that changed. This one step prevents most of the damage.
2. Export your current pages first and keep the content that ranks. If a page brings in search traffic, it earns its place on the new site, tidy or not.
3. Preserve your internal links. Rebuild the in-content links between related pages, not just the top navigation.
4. Keep the page titles and headings that already work. If a title ranks, a rewrite for style can cost you the position.
5. Crawl the new site before launch and again after, and compare. Broken links, missing pages, and orphaned content show up fast when you actually look.
6. Submit the updated sitemap and watch your search reports for the first few weeks, so a problem surfaces while it's still small.

## What recovery actually looks like

If you're reading this because traffic already dropped, the fixes are the same list run in reverse: add the redirects, restore the content, rebuild the links. What I won't promise is speed. Search engines have to recrawl the site and rebuild their trust in it, so recovery runs in weeks, sometimes longer, not days. The sooner you close the gaps, the less ground you give up, so treat a post-redesign traffic drop as urgent, not something to watch for a month.
