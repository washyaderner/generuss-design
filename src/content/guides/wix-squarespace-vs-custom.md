Wix and Squarespace are the right pick when you need a presentable site up quickly, you'll maintain it yourself, and the site's job is presence rather than serious lead generation. A custom build is the right pick when speed, conversion, and standing out from competitors decide whether the site makes money. The short version: builders are for looking professional, custom is for out-competing, and the signal to switch is when 'looks fine' stops being enough.

## What the builders do well

Give them credit, because they earn it. Wix and Squarespace let one person put a clean, professional-looking site online in a weekend with no code, no developer, and no waiting on anyone. The templates are tasteful, the hosting is handled, and the editing is visual. For a large number of small businesses that need an address on the internet more than they need a sales machine, that's the correct tool, and paying for a custom build would be a waste. Don't let anyone shame you out of the right-sized choice. A builder site that does its one job well is a better decision than a custom site that does five jobs the business didn't need, and fit is the whole thing.

## Where the ceiling bites

The ceiling shows up in three places. Speed: builders load a lot of general-purpose code to make the editor work, and that weight slows the page, which quietly costs you visitors and search ranking. Sameness: the templates that make it easy also make you look like every competitor who picked the same one, so your site stops being a reason to choose you. And conversion: builders give you layout, not persuasion, so the engineering that turns a visitor into a lead, message order, friction removal, and a clear path to contact, is simply not something a template does for you. None of these show up on launch day, which is what makes them dangerous. The site looks great, so you assume it's working, and the cost arrives quietly as visitors who leave and searches you never appear in. [Where templates hit their ceiling](/guides/website-templates-limits) goes deeper on each.

## The signals it's time to switch

You don't switch because custom is fancier. You switch when the builder starts costing you money you can measure. The signs: leads are worth real money now and the site isn't producing them, you've spent more than a weekend fighting settings panels, the site feels slow and a speed test confirms it, or you look at three competitors and can't tell the sites apart, yours included. When 'it looks fine' stops being the bar and 'it has to sell' becomes the bar, the tool has to change. One clear signal beats a vague feeling: pick the number that matters, whether it's leads per month or ranking for the search your customers use, and watch it. If it stays flat while everything else grows, the site is the bottleneck. [When DIY is right](/guides/when-diy-is-right) covers the other direction, when staying on a builder is still the smart call.

## Pick by the job, not the trend

There's no virtue in either choice on its own. A builder site that fits a presence job is a good decision; a custom site a hobby project didn't need is a bad one, and the reverse is just as true. Name what the site has to do, be clear-eyed about whether it's doing it, and let that decide. Trends and opinions will push you toward whatever the loudest person built last; your customer and your numbers are the only votes that count. The best answer is often boring, and boring is fine: a site that fits earns its keep whether it cost a monthly subscription or a custom build.
