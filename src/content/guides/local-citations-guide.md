A citation is any place online that lists your business name, address, and phone number: a Yelp page, a Facebook profile, an Apple Maps listing, an industry directory. The ones that matter are a small core of major platforms plus the directories specific to your trade, all kept perfectly consistent. You do not need hundreds of them, and you almost certainly do not need a monthly service to manage them.

## Consistency is the whole point

The reason citations work is not the link or the exposure; it is verification. Google sees your business listed in many places and cross-checks the details. When your name, address, and phone match everywhere, that consistency reads as a real, reliable business. When they conflict, an old address here, a former phone number there, a name with "LLC" in one place and not another, it reads as uncertainty, and uncertainty is the opposite of what a ranking system wants to reward. The mechanism is that plain: matching details build confidence, mismatched details erode it.

## The core few, plus your industry's directories

Most of the value sits in a handful of platforms nearly every business should claim and align. Google Business Profile is the anchor everything else supports. Facebook doubles as a place customers look you up. Apple Maps serves every iPhone user asking for directions. Yelp feeds data to other services beyond its own site.

On top of that core, add the directories that matter for your specific industry: the contractor and home-service listings for trades, the booking and wellness platforms for health and beauty, the reservation and food sites for restaurants. Two or three well-chosen industry directories are worth more than fifty generic ones. Your [Google Business Profile](/guides/google-business-profile-checklist) is the source of truth the rest should match.

A quick way to find where you already appear: search your business name, then search your phone number in quotes. The results surface the listings Google associates with you, including old ones you forgot and duplicates you never created. Data aggregators can generate listings automatically, so you may find a version of your business carrying a former address or a misspelled name. Those stray entries are the ones quietly undercutting your consistency, and cleaning them up is often worth more than adding anything new.

## Why more is not better

The pitch from citation services is volume: hundreds of listings, a number that sounds impressive on a report. In practice, a pile of low-quality listings does little for ranking and quietly works against you, because every extra listing is another place your details can drift out of sync. One wrong phone number copied across forty auto-generated directories is a consistency problem you created by chasing quantity. A tight set of accurate listings beats a sprawling set of sloppy ones every time.

## Audit, fix, forget

Citations are the rare part of local SEO that is mostly a one-time job, which is exactly why a recurring monthly fee for it deserves suspicion. Do it in one pass. Search your business name and find where you are already listed. Correct any address or phone that is outdated, and make the format identical everywhere, down to how you write the suite number. Claim the core platforms you are missing. Then leave it alone, and check again a couple of times a year, or whenever you move, change your number, or rebrand.

That is the right cadence: fix it once, revisit it rarely. Money that would go to a monthly citation service is almost always better spent on the levers that actually move week to week, your profile and your reviews.
