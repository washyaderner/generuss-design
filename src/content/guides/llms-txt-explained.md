An llms.txt file is a plain text file you place at the root of your website, the same way robots.txt has always sat there for search crawlers. Its job is to hand AI systems a clean, machine-readable summary of your site: what your business is, which pages matter, and the facts you want an assistant to repeat correctly. Whether you need one is a smaller question than the buzz suggests. The short version: probably worth it, rarely urgent.

## What the file actually is

Think of it as a signpost written for machines. It's not a ranking trick, and it doesn't change what human visitors see. It's a short, structured note, usually in plain markdown, that tells an AI crawler where to look and what's true, so it spends less effort guessing. If robots.txt says which doors a crawler may open, llms.txt says here is the tidy summary of what's inside. That's the whole idea, and its plainness is a feature, not a limitation.

## What goes inside it

Keep it short and accurate. A good llms.txt lists your business name and what you do, links to your most important pages with a single line describing each, and states the facts you want an assistant to get right: service area, hours, the offers that matter. It should mirror your site, never outrun it. If the file claims something your pages don't support, you've created exactly the inconsistency that makes assistants hedge, which is the opposite of the point.

## Where adoption really stands

Be clear-eyed here. llms.txt is emerging, not universal. Some tools read it, many don't yet, and no assistant is guaranteed to act on it today. Anyone promising that a single file will change how AI sees your business is overselling. The realistic case is quieter: it's a low-effort signal that can help at the margins now and, if the ecosystem standardizes on it later, positions you early instead of scrambling to catch up.

## Low cost, high option value

The reason to add one anyway is the shape of the bet. Writing an llms.txt takes minutes and costs nothing to host. If the tools that parse it become standard, you're already there. If they never do, you've lost a few minutes and nothing else. That asymmetry, almost no downside against real upside, is the entire argument. Hand-built sites make it especially easy, because you control the files directly and can keep the summary in step with the pages as they change. You can see how that build approach works on [the process page](/process); this site publishes its own llms.txt as a working example.

## So, does your site need one?

If you already have a clear site, accurate listings, and quotable content (which [AEO explains](/guides/what-is-aeo)), adding llms.txt is a sensible finishing touch. If you haven't done those basics yet, do them first: a summary file pointing at a thin, inconsistent presence doesn't help. Get the foundation right, keep your facts aligned, then publish the file as a low-cost extra.
