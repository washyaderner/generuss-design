If your website gets visitors but no leads, the problem is almost never how much traffic you have. It's what the site does with the traffic it already gets. Underperforming service sites leak in five specific places: they load too slowly, they don't say what they do fast enough, they give no reason to trust them, they make contact harder than it should be, or nothing happens after someone reaches out. Work through them in that order, because the early ones lose people before the later ones ever get a chance.

## Run the ten-minute triage

You can find your leak yourself in about ten minutes. Do these five checks in order and stop to fix at the first one that clearly fails.

**Speed.** Open your site on your phone on cell data, not office wifi. If the main content takes more than a few seconds to appear, you're losing people at second four and never seeing them go. A free speed test will confirm it with a number.

**Clarity.** Show your homepage to someone who doesn't know your business for five seconds, then take it away. Ask them what you do and what they were supposed to click. If they hesitate on either, strangers are hesitating too. [The five-second test](/guides/above-the-fold-checklist) shows what belongs on that first screen.

**Trust.** Scan your own page as a stranger would. Are there named reviews, real photos of you and your work, any proof you exist and deliver? If it's stock images and anonymous praise, visitors can't verify you, and they don't call businesses they can't verify. [Trust signals](/guides/trust-signals-website) walks the fix list.

**Friction.** Count the steps between landing and contacting you. A hidden phone number, a seven-field form, no booking link: each step sheds buyers, and the busy ones you most want are the first to give up.

**Silence.** Submit your own contact form and time what happens next. If it's a day of nothing, you're losing leads to whoever replied first, because interest cools by the hour.

## Why buying traffic first doubles the waste

The tempting move when leads are thin is to buy more visitors. If the site converts poorly, that's the most expensive thing you can do. More traffic through a leaky site just means more people falling out the same holes, except now you paid for each of them at the top. You spend twice: once for the visitor, once for the loss.

Fixing conversion works the opposite way. Every hole you close raises the value of every visitor you get afterward, paid or organic, for as long as the site runs. Doubling your conversion rate is the same as doubling your traffic, except you only pay for it once. That's why the order matters: fix what the site does with visitors before you spend a dollar getting more of them.
