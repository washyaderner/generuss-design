Most businesses that think they need a redesign need a refresh. A refresh fixes the site you already have: it makes the pages fast, rewrites the first screen so a stranger understands you, and repairs the path to booking. A redesign rebuilds from the foundation up. The refresh is cheaper, faster, and lower risk, so the right move is to rule it out first and rebuild only when a refresh can't reach the problem.

## What a refresh actually covers

A refresh isn't a coat of paint. It's a focused pass on the few things that cost you customers, done on the site you already own.

- **A speed pass.** Compress the images, cut the plugins and trackers you don't need, and clean up the code that loads first. Most slow sites are slow for boring, fixable reasons, and speed is the leak that touches every visitor before they read a word.
- **An above-the-fold rewrite.** The first screen is where a visitor decides to stay or leave. Rewriting the headline into words a customer would actually say, adding one piece of proof, and putting a single clear button up top can change results without moving a single page.
- **A contact-flow fix.** Follow your own path from landing to booked. Every extra field, every "email us" instead of a booking link, every step where a visitor has to think is a place leads fall out. Tightening that flow is often the highest-return hour in the whole refresh.

None of those three touch the site's structure, which is exactly why a refresh is quick and low risk. The pages, the URLs, and the navigation stay put; you're improving what sits on the foundation, not pouring a new one. That also marks the honest limit of a refresh: it can't add a dozen pages you never built, it can't rescue a platform that's fundamentally too slow, and it can't reposition a business that changed underneath its own website. When what you need is more site, not better site, a refresh runs out of room, and pretending otherwise just delays the rebuild you already need. A refresh is the scalpel; a rebuild is the demolition. Reach for the scalpel first.

## The cost asymmetry

A refresh and a rebuild aren't just different sizes of the same bill. They carry different risk. A refresh reuses everything you've already paid for: your page structure, your URLs, the content that ranks, the reviews embedded on the site. A rebuild resets all of it and adds a danger a refresh doesn't have, that you [lose rankings in the migration](/guides/website-redesign-seo) if URLs change without redirects. That's the asymmetry. The refresh keeps your accumulated equity and costs less; the rebuild spends more to put that equity at risk. Sometimes that trade is worth it. Often it isn't, and the businesses that skip straight to a rebuild rarely stop to ask.

## A plain decision rule

Here's the rule. Refresh if the site's structure is sound and the problems live on top of it: slow pages, weak copy, a broken booking flow, tired photos. Rebuild if the structure itself is the problem: a platform you've outgrown, an architecture that can't be made fast, or a business that repositioned so hard the old page count and layout no longer fit. Put simply, fixable on the current foundation means refresh; the foundation is the fault means rebuild.

When you're not sure which side you're on, that uncertainty is itself the answer: [get it measured](/guides/website-audit-before-redesign) before you spend, because a quote written without a diagnosis defaults to the biggest option. The whole decision, restlessness included, sits in the pillar on [when to redesign your website](/insights/website-redesign-timing). Rule out the cheap fix first, and you'll either save a lot of money or know for certain you needed the rebuild.
