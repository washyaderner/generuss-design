The map pack is the block of three businesses Google shows on a small map, above the ordinary list of blue links. Those three get the overwhelming share of calls, so the whole local game is about getting into them. The good news is that Google is not hiding the criteria. It ranks the pack on three things: relevance, distance, and prominence.

## What the pack is actually ranking

**Relevance** is how well your business matches what the person searched. This is mostly about your Google Business Profile: the category you choose, the services you list, the words you use to describe what you do. If someone searches "emergency electrician" and your profile just says "electrician," you are less relevant than the competitor who named the service.

**Distance** is how close you are to the person searching. You cannot move your business, but you can make sure Google knows exactly where you are and which areas you serve, so it places you correctly when someone nearby searches.

**Prominence** is how established and active your business looks: the number and recency of your reviews, the consistency of your information across the web, your presence in the directories Google trusts. This is the part most competitors neglect, which makes it the part where you can win.

## The checklist order that moves it

Work them in the order of leverage. First, fix relevance: claim your profile and set the exact right category, because category choice moves rankings more than almost anything else you can do in an afternoon. The [Google Business Profile checklist](/guides/google-business-profile-checklist) walks every field. Second, build prominence through reviews, steadily and with recency, because a trickle of fresh reviews outweighs a big number that went quiet. [How to get more reviews without begging](/guides/get-more-google-reviews) has the method. Third, tighten consistency so your name, address, and phone match everywhere Google looks.

## What you cannot control, and what that means

Distance is fixed, and so is the reality that a business physically closer to a given searcher has an edge for that search. That is not a reason to give up; it is a reason to focus on the two levers you do own. A profile that is more relevant and more prominent than the shop next door can out-rank a slightly closer competitor who never did the work. You are not trying to beat physics. You are trying to beat the businesses in your radius who left their profile half-finished.

There is a practical ceiling worth naming, too. If ten established competitors sit right on top of the searcher and you are five miles out, you may not crack the top three for that exact spot no matter how clean your profile is. What you can do is win the searches closer to you and the more specific searches where relevance beats raw proximity. Local visibility is not one ranking; it is thousands of small ones, and the work is collecting the winnable ones rather than conquering every point on the map.

## How long it really takes

Set the expectation plainly: this is weeks to months, not days. A complete, active profile can start climbing within a few weeks, but the review flow and the consistency signals compound slowly, which is exactly why they are hard for a competitor to fake overnight. Anyone promising you the top spot in a week is selling urgency, not results. The upside of the slow build is durability: a position earned this way does not evaporate the moment you stop paying someone.

Where does your site fit? The profile gets you into the pack; the website is what converts the click into a call, so the two are one project.
