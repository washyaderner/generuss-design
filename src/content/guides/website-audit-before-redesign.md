Audit before you redesign, every time, because the audit is what tells you whether you need a redesign at all. A real audit measures where your current site is actually losing customers and hands you a ranked list of fixes. Often that list is far shorter and cheaper than a rebuild. Paying for a redesign without auditing first is like agreeing to an engine replacement before anyone opened the hood: you might need it, but you have no way to know, and the shop selling it isn't the one to ask.

## The economics of measuring first

The reason to audit isn't diligence for its own sake. It's money. A large share of "we need a redesign" problems turn out to be a handful of specific leaks: a slow-loading page, a first screen that doesn't say what you do, a booking flow with one too many steps. Those are fixable on the site you already have, for a fraction of a rebuild. Skip the audit and you can't tell that case apart from the one that truly needs a new foundation, so you default to the biggest, most expensive option and hope. The audit converts a guess into a decision. It's the difference between buying a solution and buying the right solution.

## What a real audit covers

An audit worth the name measures, it doesn't just eyeball. Four areas carry most of the answer.

- **Speed, on a real phone.** Not how it feels on your fast laptop on office wifi. A performance measurement on a mid-range phone on a normal connection, because that's your actual visitor.
- **The first screen.** Whether a stranger can tell what you do, who it's for, and what to click within a few seconds of landing. This is the most-visited part of the site and the most common place it leaks.
- **The contact flow.** The full path from landing to booked, counting every field, click, and decision. Leads decay fast, and every step of friction sheds some of them.
- **Search visibility.** Whether the site is indexed, ranking for anything, and free of the technical faults that keep it invisible. A pretty site nobody can find has a different problem than an ugly one that ranks.

The output isn't a vibe. It's a ranked list that separates what's costing you customers from what merely looks dated but works fine, and those two piles rarely match your first guess.

## How the audit changes the quote

Here's where the audit pays for itself. Walk into a redesign conversation without one and you're quoting a rebuild against a feeling. Walk in with an audit and the conversation changes: now there's a specific problem list, and the right response is scoped to it. Sometimes that's a rebuild, and now you can tell whether a proposal actually addresses your findings or just sells a template swap. More often it's a shorter fix list, and the correct quote to accept is the small one.

A redesign proposal that arrives before anyone measured your site is not a plan. It's a default.
